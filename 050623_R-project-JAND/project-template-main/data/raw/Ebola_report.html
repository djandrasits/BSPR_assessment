Introduction: Ebola

Ebola virus (EBOV) is a member of the filoviridae familiy. Since the infection of EBOV in humans lead to severe hemorrhagic fever, often resulting in death, this virus is classified as a biosafety-level (BSL-) 4 pathogen. Therefore, research on this virus need a high-containment facility, whereas the numbers of these laboratories worldwide are very limited. Although first identified in 1976 in the Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC), the most devastating outbreak of EBOV occurred in 2014-2016 in Western Africa. For more information concerning the prevention and treatment, check out the World Health Organisation Fact Sheet for Ebola Virus disease.

How changed the confirmed cases and deaths over time? Which country displayed the most cumulative cases?

For the second dataset, the sex and respiratory symptoms will be compared. What are the proportions of boy and girls experiencing respiratory symptoms? Furthermore, the forced expiratory volume in 1 second (FEV1) will be investigated. How does it depend on other factors?

# Mutate country as factor

boxplot(ebola3cc$Cum_conf_cases ~ ebola3cc$Country, xlab = "",
        ylab = "Cumulative number of cases")

This boxplot shows the cumulative confirmed cases in Guinea, Liberia, and Sierra Leone. The bold black line thereby represents the median. Points below the lower whisker are defined as outliers. Especially for Sierra Leone, there are a lot of outliers present. This indicates a rapid increase in the confirmed Ebola cases at one point of the outbreak, underlining the enourmous health crisis in this country during that epidemics. Also for Guinea and Liberia, some outliers are seen. This also indicates a rapid increase in the cummulative confirmed cases in these countries at a certain point during the epidemics.

Conclusion

The outbreak of Ebola virus in 2014-2016 affected Sierra Leone the most. Although, in the beginning of the epidemic, there has not been many cases in this country. However, Sierra Leone reported the most cumulative confirmed cases by the declaration of the end of the epidemic in early 2016.

This corresponds to around 24.17% of male and 26.91% of female children experienced respiratory symptoms. What are the corresponding Confidence Intervals for each group?

SE_respsymp <- sqrt(p_byresp * (1 - p_byresp) / n_byresp)


lowerCI_p_respsymp <- p_byresp - qnorm(0.975) * SE_respsymp

upperCI_p_respsymp <- p_byresp + qnorm(0.975) * SE_respsymp

cbind(p_byresp , lowerCI_p_respsymp, upperCI_p_respsymp)
  p_byresp lowerCI_p_respsymp upperCI_p_respsymp
0 0.241791          0.1959409          0.2876411
1 0.269103          0.2190013          0.3192047

Considering the 95% Confidence interval, 19.59-28.76% of boys, and 21.90-31.92% of girls experienced respiratory symptoms.

Finally, there is a correlation between height and fev1, as well as height and age. The fev1 also correlates with the age, as older children usually have a bigger lung volume. Since taller children also have a higher lung capacity, the fev1 correlates with the height of the children. The sex seems to have a small correlation with the fev1. The presence of any respiratory symptoms have a negative correlation with fev1, since in children with respiratory symptoms, the fev1 is reduced.
